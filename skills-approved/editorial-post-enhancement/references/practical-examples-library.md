# Practical Examples Library

Templates for Hormozi/Ferriss‑style practical, personal examples.
Use these as starting points — always customize with real numbers and stories from your own experience.

## Format
**[Situation] → [Specific Action] → [Measurable Result] + [What This Means for the Reader]**

## Business & SaaS Examples
**Pricing experiment**: We tested three price points for our CRM tool — $49, $79, and $129/month. The $79 plan won with 32% more sign‑ups than $49 and only 8% fewer than $129. **What this means:** Don’t assume cheapest wins — test your pricing.

**Churn reduction**: We noticed users who didn’t complete the onboarding checklist churned at 42% vs 8% for those who did. We made the checklist mandatory and added a 5‑minute setup video. Churn dropped from 18% to 9% in 60 days. **What this means:** Activation beats acquisition — fix the first‑hour experience.

**Feature adoption**: Only 12% of users used our new reporting dashboard. We added a guided tour that popped up on login and showed a pre‑built report. Usage jumped to 68% in two weeks. **What this means:** Build it and they won’t come — you have to guide them there.

**Refund rate**: Our 30‑day money‑back guarantee was being abused — 15% of refunds came from users who downloaded everything and then canceled. We changed it to “7‑day trial, then no‑questions‑asked refund.” Refunds fell to 3%. **What this means:** Guardrails prevent abuse — test your guarantees.

**Support scaling**: Our support team was overwhelmed with 200+ tickets/day. We created a searchable knowledge base and added a “Did you try this?” widget that suggested articles before submitting a ticket. Ticket volume dropped 40% in one month. **What this means:** Deflect don’t just hire — empower self‑service.

## Marketing & Sales Examples
**Ad copy test**: We ran 5 headline variations for our Facebook ads. The winner (“How [Customer] Got [Result] in 30 Days”) got 2.1× the CTR of the control. **What this means:** Specific outcomes beat generic promises — quantify the transformation.

**Email sequence**: Our welcome sequence had a 45% open rate but only 8% click‑through. We moved the case study to email 2 and added a “What this means for you” box. CTR rose to 22%. **What this means:** Early value builds trust — don’t save the best for last.

**Landing page**: We tested a long‑form page (1,800 words) against a short version (800 words) for our webinar. The long version converted 31% higher because it answered every objection. **What this means:** Length works when it’s all useful — don’t cut for length’s sake.

**Webinar show‑up**: Only 35% of registrants attended our live webinar. We started sending text reminders 30 minutes before and added a “Stay to the end for a surprise bonus.” Show‑up rose to 61%. **What this means:** Fight friction — small nudges beat big promises.

**Sales call structure**: Our reps were doing 60‑minute discovery calls and closing 18%. We switched to 15‑minute “pain‑first” calls that asked: “What’s costing you the most money right now?” Close rate jumped to 29%. **What this means:** Qualify fast, solve specific — don’t boil the ocean.

## Health & Fitness Examples
**Workout consistency**: I tried going to the gym 5 days a week and failed after two weeks. I switched to “always do something” — even if it was just 10 minutes of stretching. I’ve now worked out 400+ consecutive days. **What this means:** Consistency beats intensity — show up, even if it’s small.

**Diet change**: I wanted to cut sugar but kept failing at 3 PM cravings. I started eating a protein bar at 2:30 PM every day. The cravings vanished and I’ve been low‑sugar for 11 months. **What this means:** Prevent don’t resist — address the craving before it hits.

**Strength gain**: I was stuck at 225 lb on bench press for 6 months. I added 2.5 lb every week, no matter what. Six months later I was at 250 lb. **What this means:** Progressive overload works — tiny, regular beats sporadic heroic.

**Sleep quality**: I was waking up tired despite 8 hours. I started wearing blue‑blocking glasses after 8 PM and kept my room below 65°F. My morning energy doubled in 10 days. **What this means:** Environment beats willpower — fix your surroundings.

**Injury prevention**: I kept getting lower‑back pain from deadlifts. I filmed my form and noticed my hips were rising too fast. I reduced the weight and focused on hips and bar moving together. Pain gone after 4 weeks. **What this means:** Technique beats ego — video yourself and fix the basics.

## Productivity & Personal Development Examples
**Time blocking**: My calendar was a mess of meetings and I got nothing done. I started blocking 9‑11 AM daily for “deep work” — no meetings, no email, no Slack. I shipped 3 major features in 6 weeks that had been stuck for 6 months. **What this means:** Guard your maker time — meetings murder momentum.

**Habit stacking**: I wanted to meditate daily but kept forgetting. I tied it to “after I brush my teeth” — something I never skip. I’ve meditated 400+ consecutive days. **What this means:** Anchor new habits to rock‑solid ones — don’t rely on motivation.

**Learning speed**: I needed to learn React fast for a project. I spent 2 days just building tiny components (button, input, modal) instead of trying to build the whole app. I was productive by day 3. **What this means:** Start stupidly small — mastery comes from reps, not heroic attempts.

**Decision fatigue**: I was making poor choices late in the day because I was deciding everything from what to wear to what to eat. I now wear the same outfit (jeans + black tee) and eat the same breakfast (eggs + avocado) every weekday. **What this means:** Reduce trivial decisions — save willpower for important ones.

**Writing habit**: I wanted to write a book but only wrote when I “felt inspired.” I committed to 200 bad words every morning before checking email. I finished the draft in 4 months. **What this means:** Show up daily — inspiration follows action, not the reverse.

## Real Estate & Investing Examples
**Cash flow analysis**: I almost bought a duplex that looked great on paper — $800/month positive cash flow. I factored in 8% vacancy and 15% for repairs and realized it was actually ‑$50/month. I walked away. **What this means:** Always stress‑test — vacancies and repairs eat profits.

**Renovation ROI**: I spent $15,000 on a kitchen remodel hoping it would add $30,000 to my home’s value. It only added $18,000. **What this means:** Not all renovations pay — focus on kitchens and baths, avoid over‑improving for the neighborhood.

**Stock position sizing**: I used to put 20% of my portfolio into any stock I liked. After a ‑40% loss on one bet, I switched to 2% max per position. My worst ‑draw‑drop went from 40% to 8%. **What this means:** Never bet the farm — diversification is your seatbelt.

**REIT analysis**: I looked at a retail REIT yielding 6.5%. Then I noticed 40% of its tenants were restaurants and gyms — high‑risk categories post‑pandemic. I skipped it and bought an industrial REIT at 4.8% with 90% warehouse tenants. **What this means:** Look beyond the yield — examine the tenants and lease lengths.

## Template for Creating Your Own Examples
1. **What was the situation?** (Be specific: time, place, stakes)
2. **What exactly did you do?** (Not “I tried harder” — what was the action?)
3. **What was the measurable result?** (Numbers, percentages, timeframes)
4. **What does this mean for the reader?** (One‑sentence takeaway they can apply)

## Using the Examples in Content
- **Drop‑in box**: Use a shaded background or border to call out the example.
- **Inline**: Weave it naturally into a paragraph (“For example, when we…”).
- **Callout**: Use “💡 What this means:” or “⚠️ Watch out for:” to highlight the lesson.
- **Data visualization**: Turn the result into a simple chart or table if it helps.
- **Personal voice**: Use “I” or “we” to make it feel real — never invent stories.

## Red Flags (When an Example Feels Fake)
- ❌ Vague numbers (“lots more traffic,” “much better results”)
- ❌ No timeframe (“eventually,” “over time,” “soon”)
- ❌ No specific action (“we improved it,” “we worked on it”)
- ❌ Too perfect (everything worked exactly as planned on the first try)
- ❌ No downsides or trade‑offs mentioned (real experiments have learning costs)
- ❌ Doesn’t match the author’s voice (suddenly switches from casual to formal)
