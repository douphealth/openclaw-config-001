# Content Strategy Planning

## Purpose
Turn business goals, customer language, search demand, and competitive gaps into a prioritized, cannibalization-safe content plan that supports real business outcomes.

## Use this when
- planning what content to create rather than drafting the final piece
- doing keyword research, clustering, or content gap analysis
- building topic cluster architecture
- checking for cannibalization before publishing
- prioritizing an editorial backlog
- planning affiliate or monetization-oriented content systems

## Do NOT use this for
- writing or editing individual articles (→ `conversion-copywriting` or `editorial-post-enhancement`)
- executing technical SEO fixes (→ `seo-audit-playbook` or `schema-ops`)
- one-off single-page copy improvements with no planning layer

## Do this
1. Define the business goal, audience, and content system objective.
2. Gather seed topics from customer language, product/offer goals, competitors, and search patterns.
3. Cluster keywords by shared intent and SERP overlap.
4. Classify clusters by intent: informational, commercial investigation, transactional, or skip.
5. Check existing site coverage and cannibalization risk before recommending new pages.
6. Design pillar/spoke or equivalent topic architecture where useful.
7. Score opportunities by business value, effort, and competitive opportunity.
8. Return a prioritized plan with formats, rationale, and next publishing actions.

## Core rules
- Never recommend new content without a cannibalization check.
- Do not confuse interesting topics with commercially useful topics.
- One page should target one cluster.
- Expand or consolidate existing pages when that is better than creating another page.
- Every topic recommendation should map to a business goal.

## Priority model
Prioritize using a mix of:
- demand / opportunity
- intent value
- business value
- competition difficulty
- effort required
- topical-authority contribution

## Output expectations
A strong plan should usually include:
- strategic goal
- audience/problem framing
- content pillars or cluster map
- cannibalization findings
- prioritized topic list
- recommended formats
- internal-linking implications
- implementation order

## Checks and common mistakes
- skipping cannibalization review
- treating every topic as equal priority
- recommending new pages when an existing page should be expanded
- skipping intent classification
- planning topics with no clear audience or business path
- building clusters without internal-linking logic

## Output contract
**Artifact:** content strategy document, cluster plan, or editorial priority roadmap
**Evidence:** keyword/intent rationale, cannibalization review, and prioritization logic
**Decision:** what to publish, expand, consolidate, or skip
**Next:** create briefs, route to execution skills, or schedule the editorial backlog
