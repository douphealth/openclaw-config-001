# Offer Positioning

## Purpose
Turn vague capability into a concrete, understandable offer people can actually say yes to. Make the next step obvious and the value undeniable.

## Use this when
- Clarifying, strengthening, or reframing a service, product, or monetization offer
- Positioning an audit, sprint, consulting package, lead magnet, or upsell
- The offer exists operationally but not persuasely
- Messaging is generic, audience is unclear, or the value prop is fuzzy

Do **not** use for: page-level copy (use `conversion-copywriting`), funnel structure (use `service-funnel-architecture`), or lead delivery setup (use `lead-magnet-delivery-ops`).

## Do this

### Phase 1: Who and why
1. Identify the buyer: role, context, what they've already tried.
2. Define the painful problem in the buyer's language (not yours).
3. Name the desired outcome — what changes after they say yes.
4. Establish urgency: why now vs. someday?

### Phase 2: Value proposition canvas
5. Map the offer against four axes:
   - **Job to be done**: What is the buyer trying to accomplish?
   - **Pains**: What's frustrating, costly, or risky about the current situation?
   - **Gains**: What does success look like? What would they pay to achieve?
   - **Offer fit**: How does this specific offer address pains and deliver gains?

6. Use the canvas to identify which parts of the offer matter most — lead with those.

### Phase 3: Define the offer
7. Choose the offer type: audit, sprint, service retainer, product, lead magnet, or upsell.
8. Clarify what is included, what is not, and what makes this different from alternatives.
9. Name the offer in plain language — no jargon, no internal terminology.
10. Quantify deliverables where possible: "5-page audit" > "comprehensive review."
11. Price anchor: show the value before the price (ROI framing, cost-of-inaction comparison).

### Phase 4: Sharpen
12. Tighten promise: one clear outcome the buyer gets.
13. Add proof: case studies, testimonials, before/after, or concrete examples.
14. Reduce risk: guarantee, freemium tier, or low-commitment entry point.
15. Make CTA obvious: one primary action, stated in buyer-language.

### Phase 5: Differentiate
16. Differentiate on mechanism (how it works), speed (time to result), certainty (risk reduction), specificity (narrow focus), or audience fit (built for them).
17. If the offer cannot be explained in 2 sentences, it's not ready.

**Differentiation depth ladder (pick the deepest you can truthfully claim):**
| Level | Type | Example |
|-------|------|---------|
| Surface | "We're better" | "Higher quality, better support" |
| Feature | Unique capability | "Only tool with real-time co-editing" |
| Mechanism | How it works differently | "AI-assisted, not AI-generated" |
| Outcome | Provable result | "First qualified lead in 48 hours" |
| Category | Redefine the space | "Not another CRM — a revenue OS" |

## Core rules
- Specific beats broad — solve one painful buying problem first.
- An offer should not try to be for everyone.
- Do not lead with abstract benefits and hide the actual deliverable.
- Do not bury the paid next step under generic education.
- Do not invent proof.

## Output contract
Deliver:
1. **Target buyer** — role, context, current frustration
2. **Problem / outcome framing** — buyer's words, with urgency
3. **Value proposition canvas** — job, pains, gains, offer fit
4. **Offer summary** — type, deliverables, price anchoring
5. **Positioning statement** — one sentence: for [buyer] who [problem], this offer [outcome] by [mechanism]
6. **Differentiators** — 2–3 reasons this beats alternatives, at the deepest truthful level
7. **CTA recommendation** — exact next step, copy, placement
8. **Proof gaps** — what evidence is missing and how to fill it

## Verification steps
- [ ] Offer can be explained in 2 sentences to a non-expert
- [ ] Buyer is specific (not "everyone" or "small businesses")
- [ ] Deliverables are concrete and countable
- [ ] At least one differentiator is non-trivial (not just "we're better")
- [ ] CTA is a single obvious next step
- [ ] Price anchor and risk reduction are present

## Checks and common mistakes
- Do not confuse capabilities with an offer — capabilities are what you can do, offers are what you sell.
- Do not position for everyone — if the offer fits everyone, it motivates no one.
- Do not lead with features and bury the outcome.
- Do not skip proof — unproven claims feel like every other offer.
- Do not add secondary offers before the primary one is sharp.
- Do not use internal jargon — if the buyer wouldn't say it, don't write it.
